The Undertow of Sadness

05/10/2019

I had left my home in Colombia three years ago and since then I had been living day by day and never knowing what would come six months ahead. A year in a small university city in Denmark, six months in Italy and then back for another year in Copenhagen. I found myself newly graduated, unemployed and absolutely in love with the city. A world full of possibilities waiting for a plan.

Finding a job had proven to be a difficult task and when my one way to stay in the country was to move to the other side of it and start over, that was exactly what I did. Accepting a job in a multinational company and moving to a city with 75,000 people, from which I knew exactly 0, sounded like a good enough plan. (Note that I’m pointing out the population because, while for some people it might sound like a reasonable number, for someone who grew up in a city with five million people, this was a small town). I figured I could always go back to Copenhagen (a its 777,218 people) once I had some work experience and mastered the language.

I was beaming with excitement and joy towards the unknown. Pack a few boxes, throw them in the car and drive. For the first time in my life I was living completely on my own and by my own rules. During the first week I made it a point to leave clothes all over the place and not clean up after myself, just because I could. It was liberating. I slept on an air mattress for a few nights while I waited for my order from Ikea to arrive and started my new routine. Wake up, bike to the train station, take the train, change onto a bus, enter the office and feel lost and overwhelmed with new information for the next eight hours. Then I'd go home, go to bed and repeat back. And back. And back.

This new commuting routine (30km each way every day) became my main social experience of the day. The same people, taking the same train, going the same way. Conversation was bound to happen and new connections were made. Most of us were not Danish so we all went out of our way to be social, meet people, stay busy and avoid loneliness at all costs. And I went with it for a few months, but after a while it became too much to bare. Or maybe just not enough. As a true introvert my energy comes from my alone time. I hate being with people only to avoid being alone, making conversation only to avoid silence. I craved real connection and couldn’t find it. If I had to tell one more person where I was from and how long I had been in Denmark, I would bang my head into a wall. I can’t build relationships based solely on the fear of boredom and loneliness, because for the most part, I don’t suffer from this. I can fill my days with books, movies, music, online courses, sports, gym, art. And that was why isolating myself was so easy.

Six months had passed and it all suddenly hit me. This was not temporary. This was my life now. This small town, with two bars, three restaurants with the same pizzas, three H&Ms, one MacDonald’s, two 7Eleven, my favorite cafe that was never open, one movie theater, one mall and five gyms. My life had now been reduced to a 3 kilometers radius and I felt the walls closing around me.

I can’t really point out where, when or how it started. That winter was tough. They all were. Dark and cold, I felt heavy, slow, dragging, so tired. Waking up was painful. I felt as if I had a brick pushing down on my chest. I couldn’t breathe. I would gather absolutely every bit of strength I had to push myself out of bed, and then there was nothing left to face the day. Every day. Then my mood was also dark, and cold. I couldn’t find joy in anything, or a good reason to move. And people. There was no energy for people, so I stayed away.

It’s such an underwhelming feeling, looking around and knowing that you have every reason for happiness, you are so lucky, you have so much, but you are so sad. And you can see the good, but you can’t feel it. So you tell yourself to pull it together, toughen up, be the strong woman you’re supposed to be, push forward, don’t whine. Crying is for the weak.

And weak I was, or so I felt. I woke up crying and couldn’t stop for the whole day. I had to hide in a conference room and blame my red eyes on the cold winter air.

It took me a while to make the decision to get some help. At some point I figured that talking about it might help, if not to fix it at least to understand what was happening and why. I have never been good at expressing my emotions or talking about my feelings, so this decision was already a hard one. I couldn’t bring myself to talk about all this with my friends. I didn’t want to burden them with what seemed like a child’s problem. “You’re sad. Big deal. Who isn’t”, I imagined them saying.

Some days later I went to see a doctor, who hopefully would refer me to a psychologist. She gave me a piece of paper with some questions: are you feeling down, depressed, hopeless? Check. Have you had little interest or pleasure in doing things? Check. Are you having trouble sleeping or sleeping too much? Check. Are you overeating? Check. Are you feeling tired, with little energy? Check, check, check. The verdict: I had a mild depression.

“So, what problem can I help you with?”, Karen, my new psychologist, said right after seating and saying our names. I was puzzled about the question, even though I saw it coming. My head was flooded with emotions and I didn’t know where to begin. I could only think about a bunch of little things that piled up and became my reasons for crying, and in thinking about this, my problems felt, yet again, so insignificant. I couldn’t believe I needed help, that I couldn’t deal with that on my own. I couldn’t believe I had to say it outloud. Make them real.

“I’m here because I feel so alone. I also don’t want to be with anyone”, I think I started. I told her my story, what had led me to be sitting in front of her then and I cried. She kept asking me where in my body I was feeling my emotions while telling her about my experiences. I tried telling her I didn’t, but she kept pushing. I started blurting out body parts for the sake of finishing the sessions. Don’t get me wrong, I know she meant well, and for someone else the exercise probably makes sense, but I just needed to talk and once I did, I was done with that. I wasn’t very convinced about her methods, but I was aware that it would take time and work, so I saw her a few more times after this.

Emotionally, I started feeling better, lighter. Physically, I was still dragging my feet where I needed to stand. I felt drained. It was time for another appointment, but this time I needed someone familiar, who knew me and understood my context. Who spoke my own language. I skyped with the daughter of one of my parents’ best friends, who is a psychiatrist. Our families have known each other forever and we used to play together as kids.

“We need to make your brain chemistry right again,” she said. “If you agree, I would suggest you start taking some medication. It’s a mild antidepressant, with no significant side effects and no risk of addiction. We can evaluate in a couple of months how you tolerate it and if that is the right course of treatment.”

I can’t tell if it was the placebo effect, the idea that there was some hope of getting better, but just a few days after starting the medication I could already feel a difference. My energy was slowly coming back, but more importantly, my mood was improving too. I was feeling like myself again, I was feeling present.

Since that first time some years ago now, these symptoms of fatigue will come and go, and I usually panic a little when they do because I remember how bad it can get and how it all starts with me being tired. Is it normal? Am I doing too much? Is it time to slow down? Or, am I heading back into depression? I quickly move into my self-care mode before it gets worse. Yoga, meditation, longer sleep, alone time, fresh air, candles, deep breaths. It helps.

It has been a hard thing to go through, but it has forced me to go deeper into my life now and everything before, find the root cause of all this pain and work on getting better. It has made me aware of who I am, what I want and what I need; aware of everything around me. I reflect more, feel more, feel better. It has also been a lesson on generosity and compassion towards others and on the notion that we don’t know what goes on behind the picture-perfect facades we try so hard to put up for show. We all come with some baggage, carry our own burdens and fight desperately to stay afloat. Every day is a reminder to be gentle, to be kind, to myself and others.

I have also become a firm believer in small pleasures, in looking for happiness and satisfaction in details, the small moments and gestures. In giving significance to what would otherwise just pass us by. In making everything count.

I live a fairly alone life, but that has been a choice, trying to stay true to my needs and giving myself enough space to be who I want to be, and that feeling of loneliness doesn’t get me too often. When it does, I hold on as tight as I can to the love around me, to the people who matter and the little things that give me a sense of peace and make me smile. It often comes during the colder and darker months and I know to prepare for this. Make sure I’m emotionally strong for when this arrives so that it doesn’t wash me over and drown me with its waves. Make sure I can resurface and stand strong without too much sorrow.
